1. **Evaluate Comorbidities Holistically**: Consider the interplay of multiple diagnoses and their potential to indicate risk factors or complications. For example, a patient with diabetes and hypertension should be assessed for cardiovascular disease risk, even if not explicitly diagnosed.

2. **Prioritize Stronger Clinical Indicators**: Focus on well-established clinical indicators (e.g., medications like insulin for diabetes, or antihypertensives for hypertension) rather than relying solely on less definitive signals. Ensure that the presence of these indicators is weighted appropriately in predictions.

3. **Identify Patterns of Complications**: Recognize that certain conditions (e.g., diabetes, chronic kidney disease) often lead to specific complications (e.g., cardiovascular disease, stroke). Use these associations to inform predictions, especially when multiple related conditions are present.

4. **Avoid Overreliance on Single Diagnoses**: Do not assume that the absence of a specific diagnosis (e.g., obesity) negates the risk for related conditions. Instead, consider the overall clinical picture, including medications and other comorbidities.
