1. **Evaluate Comorbidities Holistically**: Consider the interplay of multiple diagnoses (e.g., diabetes, hypertension, and hyperlipidemia) as they often co-occur and can indicate a higher risk for complications or disorders, rather than relying on single diagnoses.

2. **Prioritize Recent Clinical Events**: Give more weight to recent hospital visit records and procedures performed, as they may indicate acute changes in the patient's health status that could affect predictions for future conditions.

3. **Identify Patterns in Medication Use**: Analyze prescribed medications for indications of underlying conditions (e.g., insulin for diabetes, statins for hyperlipidemia) to support predictions, recognizing that the presence of certain medications can be a strong signal for associated disorders.

4. **Utilize Diagnostic Combinations**: Look for combinations of diagnoses that may not individually indicate a condition but collectively suggest a higher risk (e.g., a patient with both hypertension and coronary artery disease may be at risk for cardiovascular events).

5. **Avoid Rigid Diagnostic Criteria**: Do not require explicit diagnoses for all positive predictions; instead, consider risk factors and clinical indicators that suggest a likelihood of conditions, allowing for more nuanced predictions.
